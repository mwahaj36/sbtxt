# Abstract

### Subtext: A Hybrid Semantic Discovery Engine using Intent Decomposition, Keyword-Enriched Embeddings, and Asymmetric Additive Scoring

Traditional movie discovery systems primarily rely on lexical keyword matching (titles, genres) or collaborative filtering (recommendations), both of which struggle to capture the nuanced "vibe" or narrative essence of a user's intent. While modern Large Language Model (LLM) embeddings have enabled semantic vector search, these systems often suffer from "intent dilution"—where specific entities like actors or titles are lost in the overall semantic average, and complex queries are flattened into a single, less-precise vector. A further challenge is the "vocabulary gap" between film eras: a 1952 plot synopsis and a 2016 one use fundamentally different language to describe thematically identical films, causing pure vector similarity to fail on cross-era discovery.

In this paper, we present **Subtext**, a high-precision discovery engine designed to bridge the gap between "vibe-based" thematic exploration and entity-specific retrieval. Subtext utilizes an evolutionary search architecture that transitions from pure vector similarity to a hybrid model featuring **Intent Decomposition**, **Keyword-Enriched Embeddings**, **Multi-Pool Retrieval**, and **Asymmetric Additive Scoring**. Our approach embeds movies using a rich vibe string combining overview, TMDB keywords, genres, cast, and reviews—bridging the cross-era vocabulary gap. At query time, we decompose natural language queries into distinct sub-intents, retrieve candidates through complementary pools (vector similarity, genre DNA, and keyword siblings), and rank them with a transparent, penalty-based scoring formula that preserves natural cosine similarity spread.

We evaluate Subtext across 94 iterations of the engine (V0 through V94). Through systematic ablation of scoring components—including the removal of dead-band compression, hardcoded retrieval hacks, and unused re-ranking models—we demonstrate that a clean, semantically-driven architecture with deterministic keyword overlap signals significantly outperforms over-engineered alternatives. Our findings suggest that embedding enrichment at index time, combined with multi-pool retrieval and penalty-based scoring, is a superior paradigm for discovery-heavy applications where the user seeks a specific "feeling" rather than a specific filename.
